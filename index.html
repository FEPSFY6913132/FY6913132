Abstract

        Alan Turing first developed the Imitation Game in hopes to answer the question, “Can machines think?” It was in 1950, that Turing officially 
        posed the question in his research paper, “Computing Machinery and Intelligence,” however since then, many technological advances have been 
        made. This paper raises the question of where AI sits on the scale of intelligence. Through my analysis, it has shown that we first need to 
        define intelligence. Once we have this understanding, we can then evaluate behaviour and determine if it is ‘intelligent’. Presently, while 
        machines can perform ‘intelligent’ tasks, the process they use to come to execute them cannot necessarily be considered ‘thinking.’ However, 
        with the current trajectory of AI progress, the possibility of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) could change the way we perceive 
        intelligence.

Introduction

        The Turing Test assesses a computer’s ability to behave indistinguishably from a human (Mohn, 2023). Turing predicted that by 2000, a computer 
        “would be able to play the Imitation Game so well that the average interrogator will not have more than a 70% chance of making the right 
        identification after 5 minutes of questioning.” It is 25 years later, and no computer has ever come close to achieving this (Britannica, 2025). 
        Does that speak to the intelligence of modern computers or to the relevance of the Test? Develops in artificial intelligence all seem to move 
        towards making it more human-like. If something acts like a sentient being, can it be considered sentient (Britannica, 2025), even if that 
        something is a machine?

The Imitation Game

        The Imitation Game was originally a thought experiment, designed by Alan Turing (Carnegie Mellon University, n.d.). It was laid out in his paper 
        ‘Computing Machinery and Intelligence’ in 1950. Originally, the basis of the experiment was a remote human interrogation with a fixed time frame 
        (Britannica, 2025). The same principles were then applied to a machine, where an interrogator must successfully distinguish between a man and a 
        computer based on their responses (Britannica, 2025). This adapted version is better known today, as the ‘Turing Test’ 
        (Carnegie Mellon University, n.d.).
